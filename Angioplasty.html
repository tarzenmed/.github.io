Angioplasty

  1. Home
  2. Angioplasty

Coronary angioplasty, also known as percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), is a medical procedure used to treat coronary artery disease (CAD). It involves the use of a thin, flexible tube called a catheter that is inserted into a blood vessel, typically in the groin or wrist, and threaded to the coronary arteries. Once in position, a small balloon attached to the catheter is inflated at the site of a narrowed or blocked coronary artery. This action compresses the plaque and widens the passage, restoring blood flow to the heart muscle. In many cases, a stent (a small mesh-like tube) is also placed at the site to help keep the artery open. Coronary angioplasty aims to improve blood flow to the heart, alleviating symptoms of angina (chest pain) and reducing the risk of heart attacks. It is a minimally invasive alternative to open-heart surgery for certain cases of coronary artery disease. The procedure is performed by a specialized interventional cardiologist in a cardiac catheterization lab, often with the patient under sedation.

Coronary angioplasty has become a widely utilized and highly effective intervention in cardiology, contributing significantly to the management of coronary artery disease and the improvement of patients' quality of life.

How is the Procedure Performed?

Detailed explanation of how a Coronary Angioplasty procedure is performed:

1. Preparation of the Patient:
  • The patient is positioned on an examination table in a cardiac catheterization lab.
  • area where the catheter will be inserted (usually the groin or wrist) is cleaned and numbed with a local anesthetic.
2. Insertion of the Catheter:
  • pecialized interventional cardiologist makes a small incision and inserts a thin, flexible tube called a catheter into the artery. The choice of entry point (groin or wrist) depends on the preference and expertise of the cardiologist.
3. Guiding the Catheter to the Heart:
  • Using X-ray guidance, the cardiologist carefully threads the catheter through the arterial system, advancing towards the heart. This allows them to reach the coronary arteries.
4. Contrast Injection:
  • Once the catheter is in position near the heart, a contrast dye (iodine-based) is injected through the catheter. This dye helps to visualize the coronary arteries on X-ray images.
5. Identifying the Blockage:
  • The X-ray images (angiograms) reveal the location and severity of any blockages in the coronary arteries. This information guides the cardiologist in planning the intervention.
6. Balloon Inflation:
  • A small balloon, located at the tip of the catheter, is positioned at the site of the blockage. The balloon is then inflated. This action compresses the plaque against the walls of the artery, widening the passage and restoring blood flow.
7. Stent Placement (If Needed):
  • In some cases, a stent is used along with the angioplasty. A stent is a mesh-like tube made of metal or other materials. It is expanded at the site of the blockage to help keep the artery open.
8. Balloon Deflation and Catheter Removal:
  • After the balloon has been inflated and, if used, the stent has been placed, the balloon is deflated and the catheter is carefully withdrawn.
9. Post-Procedure Monitoring:
  • The patient is observed for a period of time to ensure there are no immediate complications.
10. Recovery and Follow-Up:
  • After the procedure, the patient is typically monitored for a few hours before being discharged. They are advised to rest and refrain from strenuous activities for a short period.
11. Follow-Up Care:
  • Patients are scheduled for follow-up appointments to monitor their progress and discuss any further treatment or lifestyle modifications that may be necessary.
  • Coronary angioplasty is a well-established and effective procedure for restoring blood flow to the heart and alleviating symptoms of coronary artery disease. While it is generally considered safe, all medical procedures carry some level of risk, which is carefully assessed by the medical team before proceeding with the angioplasty.

How is the Procedure Performed?

The right doctor to consult for Coronary Angioplasty is actually an Interventional Cardiologist.

Interventional Cardiologist: An Interventional Cardiologist is a specialized cardiologist with advanced training in minimally invasive procedures involving the heart and blood vessels. They are specifically trained to perform procedures like angioplasty. They have expertise in navigating catheters through blood vessels to reach the heart and perform interventions.



What is the Usual Success Rate for this Procedure?

The success rate for Coronary Angioplasty, also known as percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), typically falls within a high range of success, generally between 82% to 94%. This means that in the majority of cases, the procedure is effective in opening blocked or narrowed coronary arteries, thus restoring blood flow to the heart muscle.

However, it's important to acknowledge that, like any medical procedure, there are potential risks and complications associated with Coronary Angioplasty. These may include:

  • Heart Attack: While rare, a heart attack can occur during the procedure. This can happen if there is a dislodgement of plaque or a blood clot forms during the intervention.
  • Coronary Artery Damage: In some cases, the manipulation of catheters within the coronary arteries may cause damage. This is a known risk, although it is relatively uncommon.
  • Kidney Problems: The contrast dye used in the procedure can potentially cause kidney issues, particularly in patients with pre-existing kidney conditions.
  • Stroke: Although rare, there is a small risk of stroke associated with the procedure. This risk can be minimized with careful monitoring and technique.
  • Abnormal Heart Rhythms: Some patients may experience irregular heart rhythms, known as arrhythmias, during or after the procedure. These are typically monitored and managed by the medical team.
  • Bleeding or Hematoma: There is a slight risk of bleeding at the insertion site, especially if the patient is on blood-thinning medications.
